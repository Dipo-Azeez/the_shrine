History

Discover the rich history of America’s Catholic Church

The History and Heritage of America’s Catholic Church

The United States has long been considered the great melting pot, where people from many diverse lands have come to dwell. The Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception is proud to reflect this rich heritage of America.

Home to more than 80 chapels and oratories honoring the Mother of God and representing peoples from every corner of the globe, the National Shrine reflects the ethnic and cultural diversity of the United States and the unity and universality of the Catholic Church.

Built by generations of faithful American Catholics to honor the patroness of our nation—the Blessed Virgin Mary under her title of the Immaculate Conception — the National Shrine is affectionately referred to as America’s Catholic Church.

Among the nationalities and ethnicities represented throughout the Basilica are African, Austrian, Chinese, Cuban, Czech, Filipino, French, German, Guamanian, Hungarian, Indian, Irish, Italian, Korean, Latin American, Lebanese, Lithuanian, Maltese, Polish, Slovak, Slovenian, and Vietnamese.

Religious communities from around the world are also represented, including the Augustinians, Carmelites, Claretians, Dominicans, Franciscans, Jesuits, Montfort Missionaries, Oblates of Mary Immaculate, Redemptorists, Salesians, Sisters of Charity, Sisters of Providence, and Vincentians.

The Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception truly is the embodiment of the people who are the fabric of the Catholic faith and mosaic of our great nation.

Saint John Paul II, the first pope to visit the National Shrine, perhaps best expressed its essence:

“This Shrine speaks to us with the voice of all America, with the voice of all the sons and daughters of America, who have come here from the various countries…. When they came, they brought with them in their hearts the same love for the Mother of God that was characteristic of their ancestors and of themselves in their native lands. These people, speaking different languages, coming from different backgrounds of history and traditions in their own countries, came together around the heart of a Mother they all had in common.” (October 7, 1979)

The History of the National Shrine

Affectionately referred to as America’s Catholic Church, the Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception has been a century in the making.

Although its foundation stone was laid in 1920, this great shrine seems to have been conceived of as early as 1846, the year the Bishops of America declared the Blessed Virgin Mary the patroness of the United States under her title of the Immaculate Conception. That year, the Lowell Courier Journal, a newspaper in Massachusetts, wrote of “a magnificent Catholic Church to be built at Washington, D.C. after the manner of the great cathedrals of the Old World from subscriptions of every Catholic Parish in America.”

Through the generosity of generations of American Catholics, the Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception stands as our nation’s preeminent Marian shrine and patronal church, rivaling the great sanctuaries of Europe and the world, not only in size and stature, but also in beauty, dignity and sanctity.

National Shrine History Timeline

Explore the remarkable history of the Basilica of the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception from its earliest beginnings to present day. This interactive historical timeline highlights the important moments and major milestones of America’s Catholic Church.

1846

Sixth Council of Baltimore

At the Sixth Council of Baltimore, the American Bishops resolve to petition the Holy See to place the United States under the patronage of Mary under the title of her Immaculate Conception (as depicted in the bas-relief on the Basilica’s façade pictured here).

Loading

1847

Pope Pius IX officially names the Blessed Virgin Mary, under her title of the Immaculate Conception, Patroness of the United States

This was in response to the petition of the American Bishops at the Sixth Council of Baltimore.

Loading

1910

Monsignor Thomas J. Shahan, Rector of The Catholic University of America, proposes building a national shrine in honor of the Blessed Virgin Mary

Monsignor Shahan envisioned a national shrine similar to the great churches of Europe. A tireless advocate for this effort, he eventually founds the National Shrine of the Immaculate Conception.

Loading

1913

Monsignor Shahan travels to Rome to share his dream with Pope Pius X

After sharing his plans with the Holy Father, Monsignor Shahan received Pope Pius’ blessing to proceed with the building of the Shrine.

Loading Image

1913

Pope Pius X (Saint Pius X) approves the plan for the building of a national shrine in honor of the Patroness of the United States

Not only did Pope Pius X wholeheartedly approve Monsignor Shahan’s suggestion but the pope also enthusiastically made a personal donation officially helping to launch the ambitious project.

Loading Image

1914

Monsignor Shahan is named a bishop by Pope Pius X

Monsignor Shahan is named a bishop by Pope Pius X. Newly ordained, Bishop Shahan continues to pursue his vision of the National Shrine.

May 16, 1920

Blessing of the Land

At 9:30 a.m. on May 16, 1920, Archbishop Giovanni Bonzano, Apostolic Delegate to the United States, celebrated Mass and blessed the site of the future National Shrine. More than 6,000 people attended the event, including 1,500 Knights of Columbus and 500 Daughters of Isabella. This significant event marked the beginning of the National Shrine’s construction process.

Loading Image

September 23, 1920

James Cardinal Gibbons, the son of Irish Immigrants and Archbishop of Baltimore, lays the Foundation Stone for the National Shrine

After several years of planning, work begins with the laying of the Foundation Stone. Excitement and support spreads nationwide as the project gets underway.

September 23, 1920

The Foundation Stone is laid for the National Shrine by James Cardinal Gibbons

Surrounded by a joyous crowd of more than a thousand Catholics, the Archbishop of Baltimore lays the Foundation Stone on the site of the National Shrine.

April 20, 1924

First public Mass is celebrated in the Crypt Church

First public Mass is celebrated on Easter Sunday at the National Shrine.

Loading

April 20, 1924

The Crypt Church is the site of the National Shrine’s first public Mass

The first public Mass was celebrated on Easter Sunday in the Crypt Church of the National Shrine.

Loading

1931

Dedication of the Chapel of Our Lady of Lourdes

The Chapel of Our Lady of Lourdes is the first chapel dedicated outside of the Crypt Church.

Loading

1931

The Crypt Level of the National Shrine is completed

Although the Crypt Level was completed, construction for the rest of the National Shrine came to a halt due to the death of Bishop Shahan, the years of the Great Depression, and the events leading up to and beyond WWII.

Loading

November 15, 1954

Construction resumes on the Upper Church and superstructure of the National Shrine

Renewed construction began during the Marian Year 1953-1954 largely due to the efforts of Bishop John F. Noll of Fort Wayne, Chairman of the Episcopal Committee for the National Shrine.

Loading

November 15, 1954

After WWII, work on the Shrine is renewed at last

With the help and efforts of Catholics from across the country, the site of the National Shrine becomes busy with new construction and workers.

Loading

November 20, 1959

Dedication of the National Shrine

Thousands gathered for the blessing and dedication of the National Shrine upon the completion of the superstructure of the Great Upper Church, nearly 40 years after the foundation stone was placed. Ornamentation and embellishment of the National Shrine has continued ever since.

Loading

November 20, 1959

After so many years, the Upper Church is completed and the Shrine is dedicated

The dedication of the National Shrine was celebrated in Catholic parishes across the United States.

Loading

July 1963

The bells for the carillon in the Knights Tower are consecrated and hoisted in the 329-foot tower

The tower and carillon were gifts of the Knights of Columbus. The bell tower makes the National Shrine the second tallest building in Washington, DC, after the Washington Monument. The Christening of the Bells was on July 14 and the installation was on July 23.

Loading

1972

Mother Teresa (Saint Teresa of Calcutta) makes her first visit to the National Shrine

This would be the first of several visits by the saint, Noble Peace Prize winner, and “honorary American citizen” to the National Shrine. Mother Teresa would visit the National Shrine many times before her death in 1997. She is depicted in a heroic-size statue in the Hall of American Saints and will also be depicted in the Trinity Dome procession of saints.

Loading

1973 — 1997

Mother Teresa was a frequent visitor to the National Shrine

Not only did Mother Teresa visit the Shrine several times, but many of her Missionaries of Charity make their vows at the Shrine.

Loading

October 7, 1979

Pope John Paul II (Saint John Paul II) visits the National Shrine; he is the first pope to do so

Saint John Paul II visited the National Shrine three times during his life, twice as the Cardinal-Archbishop of Krakow and once as Pope. During his final visit he eloquently outlined the ethos of the National Shrine: “The Shrine speaks to us with the voice of all America, with the voice of all the sons and daughters of America, who have come here from various countries of the Old World. When they came, they brought with them in their hearts the same love of the Mother of God that was characteristic of their ancestors and of themselves in their native lands. These people speaking different languages, coming from different backgrounds of history and traditions in their own countries, came together around the heart of a mother they all had in common.”

Loading

October 7, 1979

Pope John Paul II becomes the first pope to visit the National Shrine

Pope John Paul II's visit to the Shrine is greeted by loud cheers from everyone.

November 2005

Work begins for the installation of mosaics for the North and South Nave Domes: The Redemption Dome and The Incarnation Dome

The Redemption Dome is finished in 2006, and then work is begun on the Incarnation Dome. The latter is finished and dedicated in 2007.

Loading

April 16, 2008

Pope Benedict XVI visits the National Shrine and celebrates Solemn Vespers

Pope Benedict XVI expressed great happiness on visiting Mary’s Shrine, saying, “…it gives me great joy to greet you today at the start of my visit to this great country...in this Basilica dedicated to the Immaculate Conception of the Blessed Virgin Mary, a shrine of special significance to American Catholics, right here in the heart of your capitol city.” During his visit the pope also bestowed “a Golden Rose for Our Mother Mary,” an honor that goes back to the eleventh century.

Loading

April 16, 2008

Pope Benedict XVI entering the inside of the National Shrine

The crowds respond with joy and excitement at the pope's entrance into the upper church.

Loading

April 16, 2008

Pope Benedict XVI views The Redemption and The Incarnation Domes, as well as the future Trinity Dome

Archbishop Wuerl and Monsignor Rossi point out the three domes to Pope Benedict.

Loading

November 20, 2009

50th Anniversary of the Dedication of the National Shrine

50th Anniversary of the 1959 Dedication of the National Shrine

Loading

August 29, 2015

The newest chapel of the National Shrine is dedicated to Our Lady of Hungary

A gift of Hungarian Catholics, the Chapel of Our Lady of Hungary is the 81st chapel be added to the National Shrine.

Loading

September 23, 2015

On his first papal visit to the United States, Pope Francis visits the National Shrine on the 95th anniversary of the laying of the Foundation Stone

Pope Francis becomes the third pope to visit the Shrine and the first pope to celebrate Mass there. He also presides over the first canonization ever to take place on American soil, that of Saint Junípero Serra in an outdoor Mass. Pope Francis also blesses the Credo or “I believe” segment of the mosaic that will encircle the Trinity Dome when it is completed. Once the Trinity Dome is finished, it will mark the official end of the construction of the National Shrine’s Great Upper Church.

Loading

September 23, 2015

Pope Francis' visit to the National Shrine

Pope Francis celebrates the canonization Mass of St. Junípero Serra outside the National Shrine.

Loading

December 8, 2017

Dedication of the Trinity Dome

The Trinity Dome Mosaic is dedicated, marking the completion of the National Shrine according to its original architectural and iconographic plans. Described as the crowning jewel of America’s Catholic Church, the Trinity Dome mosaic features the Most Holy Trinity, the Blessed Virgin Mary under her title of the Immaculate Conception, a procession of saints, and the four evangelists, encircled by the Nicene Creed.
